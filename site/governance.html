Governance

How AbyssBSD changes

A system you can hold in your head needs a process you can hold in your head. AbyssBSD has one: formal enough to be honest, small enough to stay out of the way.

Formal informal

There is structure here, not bureaucracy. A small RFC process governs the decisions that genuinely deserve deliberation, and nothing else. Everything below the threshold, a bug fix, a refactor, a better error message, just ships through ordinary review. The point of writing the process down is not ceremony; it is so that the boundary between "send a patch" and "write a proposal" is a rule, not a mood.

Guided by a BDFL, for now

While AbyssBSD is young, it is guided by a benevolent dictator for life: James Kane, the project's initiator and architect, who holds final authority over the AbyssBSD layer and renders the decision on every RFC. The reason is speed and coherence. A desktop with this many simultaneously-moving pieces, bus, broker, compositor, toolkit, shell, needs decisions that fit together, and a committee at this scale produces churn and weakly-defended compromises instead.

The role is honest about its cost: it is one person's worldview. That is defensible while the project is small and untenable once it is not, so the process names, precisely, the moment it ends.

What needs an RFC

An RFC is required for changes to the load-bearing surfaces: the unified message primitive and the wire format, the object-capability model, the public surface and its versioned epochs, the component interface schemas, the toolkit API, the performance and memory budgets, and the dependency allowlist. Changes to the FreeBSD base itself are not AbyssBSD's to make; they are filed upstream with FreeBSD, because the base is kept whole and never forked.

Everything else stays light. Bug fixes, internal refactors, diagnostic improvements, documentation, and additive changes that follow an established pattern do not need an RFC at all. When in doubt, a one-page pre-RFC sketch gets a yes, a no, or a "just send the patch" within about two weeks.

The lifecycle

An RFC moves through a fixed set of stages, each with a published target time so nothing stalls in silence:

  • Pre-RFC. A short sketch. A commitment to respond, not to accept.
  • Draft. The proposal is written up; a shepherd is assigned to edit it toward readiness.
  • Discussion. Open to public comment for at least two weeks.
  • Decision. The BDFL accepts, rejects, defers, or records a withdrawal. There are no surprise rejections: a likely no is signalled in the thread first.
  • Implementation. Accepted is not done. The work follows the project's phase-exit-gate pattern: an end-to-end test, a negative test, the design docs updated, and the change proven to sit inside the budgets.
  • Stabilised. A change to the public surface stays fluid until it is folded into a numbered epoch; from then it can change only through a new RFC and a real migration path.

The invariants

Some properties are load-bearing for what AbyssBSD is. An RFC may still propose to change one, but it starts at a strong presumption against, and it has to argue the invariant itself in the open, not smuggle the change through. The shepherd rejects silent invariant changes.

  • The FreeBSD base is never forked. It is kept whole and tracked upstream.
  • One message primitive. A single typed message carries UI events, IPC, the display protocol, and capabilities. There is no second bus, no D-Bus.
  • Capabilities, not ambient authority. A process is born holding nothing; authority travels only as unforgeable handles.
  • No X11, no Wayland in the base. The compositor is from-scratch; any compatibility layer is optional and out of tree.
  • Zero vendored dependencies. The AbyssBSD layer leans on the Rust standard library and a small, version-controlled allowlist.
  • Budgets are walls. The idle-desktop memory budget and the input-to-photon latency budget are build failures when exceeded, not aspirations.
  • Written in Rust, on stable. No nightly-only features in shipped code.
  • Hold it in your head. Surface area is a cost; a change that grows the system without a matching simplification has to justify itself.

The catch-fire trigger

The BDFL model fits a project with no independent downstreams and few outside contributors. AbyssBSD is a whole operating system, not a part other projects consume, so its version of catching fire is a real community forming around it: people running it, people building on it, and people shipping applications for it.

Three conditions measure that, each written to need genuine, independent traction rather than a flash in the pan:

  • Independent downstream systems. Distributions, redistributions, or substantial forks built on the AbyssBSD layer by distinct teams, in real use.
  • Sustained external contribution. Several contributors from outside the BDFL's organisation, each landing accepted RFCs, over a rolling year.
  • An external application ecosystem. Published AbyssBSD apps by outside authors that an independent user base depends on.

When two of the three hold for a sustained six months, the governance document is revised: a steering committee forms, and the BDFL becomes Lead Architect, keeping a veto over the design invariants above but losing unilateral authority over everything else. The transition is planned for in advance, not improvised when it arrives.

The full process lives in GOVERNANCE.md at the repository root, with the RFC format, the template, and the precise timings and thresholds. This page is the summary.
